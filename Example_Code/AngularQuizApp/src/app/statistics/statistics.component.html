A BRIEF HISTORY OF FAKE NEWS

In 2017 "fake news" was Collins Dictionary word of the year and whilst we now might find ourselves in a post-truth age, there has never really has been an age of truth. Outright lies or misrepresented information have been used throughout time to alter views and opinions, and make people question what the truth is. Whatever form it comes in, fake news has been present throughout time.

2000 years ago, Octavian, a relative of Julius Caesar, understood he needed the support of the public to become an effective ruler. During a civil war with one of Rome's greatest generals, Mark Anthony, Augustus launched a campaign targeting the general, disseminating lies that he was the antithesis of a true Roman, as he did not hold their prized values, such as faithfulness and respect, in high regard. Not only did Augustus spread these lies throughout Rome by the use of public poetry and slogans printed on coins, he also potentially fabricated Mark Anthony's official will and testament which claimed that he would leave Roman territories to the children of the Egyptian ruler, Cleopatra, of whom Roman's were virulently critical of. The result of this fake news campaign was that Mark Anthony was branded a traitor by the the Roman senate and it ultimately led to the destruction of the Roman Republic and the rise of the Roman Empire with Octavian, now called Augusts, as it's first emperor.


One of humanities greatest inventions, the printing press, allowed for the mass production and spread of information and news. Unfortunately this also included fake news.

One instance of this new form of fake news occurred in Great Britain during the 18th Century at the height of the Jacobite rebellion. King George II was targeted by rebel publishers who, in one attempt to destabilise the establishment, reported that the king was ill. Whilst these falsehoods originated from within the rebels, the news was republished by more reputable printers, leading to the King's reputation being tarnished.

Since the invention of the printing press in the mid 15th Century and the creation of the first newspaper in 1605, newspapers have become an easy way to disseminate news and information to the public.

In 1835, a newspaper, The New York Sun, printed a number of articles purporting that life and even civilisation, had been discovered on the moon. The articles described creatures such as unicorns and flying bat-men, and described how there were trees, oceans, and beaches on the moon. The newspaper wrongly attributed these discoveries to one of the most well known astronomers of the time, Sir John Herschel. In detail, the article described the new scientific methods that had been used in the discoveries, and this not only convinced people that there was in fact life on the moon, but that they should also subscribe to the New York Sun. Once the newspaper had achieved their aim of increasing subscribers, they announced that the reports of life on the moon had simply been a hoax.


Throughout warfare, fake news, or if used in a political capacity, propaganda, has been used in an attempt to change people's views. By convincing people that the other side is their enemy or that they should go to war, propaganda has proven to be an effective tool utilised by governments to sway public opinion.

In 1898 the USS Maine, a United States Navy ship, sank in the Havana Harbour in Cuba, following an explosion. At that time Cuba was a Spanish territory. Two main theories on what caused the ship to sink emerged, with one camp saying it was due to a mine and the other arguing it was caused by a spontaneous fire in the ship's magazine. Even without any evidence, some American newspapers, in a bid to increase subscriber count, claimed that Spain was responsible for the ship sinking. With dramatic illustrations used to convince readers, many Americans came to see the Spanish as the enemy and two months later the Spanish-American War began.

During World War One, two London newspapers, the Times and the Daily Mail, published articles stating that Germany had 'Kadaver' factories which, according to the newspapers' "anonymous sources", were extracting fat from the bodies of dead soldiers in order to make margarine and soap. Once the war had ended, it came to light that the story had come from MI7, part of an official government department. Despite knowing that it was a lie, they used the story to convince the public that the war was just, as they were fighting against a barbaric enemy.


Despite the proliferation of falsehoods and misinformation throughout time, the term "fake news" has really only come into the public mind in the last 5 years. With the ever increasing presence of social media and thus the ease at which stories which haven't been fact-checked can spread, there has been an explosion of fake news. Stories can reach millions within minutes and billions within hours, all without being fact-checked.

THE PROBLEM WITH FAKE NEWS

Many publishers who create and share these stories are simply in it for "the clicks", as the more traffic that their websites gain, the more money they can make. This is no different to the newspapers that published the fake moon stories, or those who blamed the Spanish for the sinking of the USS Maine, their aim was to gain more subscribers, with this "clickbait" appealing to many due to sensationalised headlines. Some are more insidious and want to sway public opinion by spreading news that appeals to certain beliefs. This is especially poignant during the US elections, especially in 2016, when fake news about certain candidates was spread and potentially impacted the way people voted. Some politicians and influential individuals began to call legitimate news fake, which can lead people to be confused about what is true and in turn can lead to the proliferation of actual fake news.

With each historical instance of fake news outlined above, a specific problem with fake news, of which there are multitudes, can be extracted. With Octavian, who through a combination of factors, including fake news, became the first Roman emperor Augustus, the clear problem is that officials who reside at the pinnacle of power are willing and able to use fake news in order to propagate and further their personal aims. With millions looking up to these people, whether it be a Roman emperor the Prime Minister of the UK, or the President of the United States, they can use the platform given to them by the people, to spread misinformation and lies for selfish gains. There are many who would believe those with such power and influence would not, or do not need to spread fake news, however if the previous 5 years have taught the world anything, it should be that no one, even the "leader of the free world", are infallible.


As with King George in the 1700s, one major problem with fake news is that despite many of these stories not being fact-checked, they are still shared by individuals and organisations who believe they are true and if they have a large following these falsehoods can often be taken more seriously and spread further, leading to the wider dissemination of fake news. This has been especially poignant during the COVID-19 pandemic, as conspiracy theorists have used their opportunity to spread false information about the causes. One such target, is 5G. Despite there being no links between 5G and coronavirus, there have been multiple attacks on 5G masts and the UK government has been forced to address the issue, with the theories being called "dangerous nonsense". Whilst much of this fake news was initially spread via social media between non-influential individuals, there have been numerous celebrities with a reach of millions who have furthered such unsubstantiated claims. The problem with this is that what these influential individuals say is often believed by their followers without any second guessing.

Much like with the printing press and social media, technology has had an active role in making the spread of fake news much easier. What was once thought of as a relatively reliable source of news was video, which is due to the difficulties people had faking them. Yet, due to the creation of a new technology, deepfakes, the reliability of video sources is now more easily questioned. A deepfake is a video that has been generated by computer software. Many current examples of deepfakes involve placing the head of someone famous onto another's body. Whilst many simply involve having the deepfake sing a song, as the technology becomes more developed and understood, it could lead to a new generation of fake news in which "deepfaked" politicians are made to say whatever the creator wants and the fakes will become harder to detect.

WHAT YOU CAN DO

Due to the platform and reach that many celebrities and politicians hold, they should be held to a higher standard when it comes to the information that they spread. Just because someone is your favourite artist or sports personality, that does not mean they cannot be wrong.
